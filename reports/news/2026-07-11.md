<b>═ 🔥 TOP STORY ═</b>
Binarly's research team has disclosed six new vulnerabilities (BRLY-2026-037 through BRLY-2026-042) in U-Boot's FIT image signature-verification code, all triggered while the bootloader is still parsing an untrusted image — before the signature check that's supposed to establish the chain of trust ever runs. Two of the six enable arbitrary code execution at boot (a null-pointer/stack overflow in fdt_find_regions, and a stack buffer underflow from a failed fdt_get_name call); the other four are memory-safety bugs that crash or hang the device. The affected code paths trace back to U-Boot v2013.07, meaning over 50 stable releases and every vendor fork built on top of them — including the U-Boot-based bootloaders under embedded Linux boards, BMCs, and industrial controllers — are potentially exposed. No CVEs have been assigned yet, and there's no patch to point to; this is a "know your exposure" moment, not a "go patch now" one. Why it matters: this is the bootloader layer, the one component every secure-boot chain assumes is trustworthy — a bug here undermines everything built on top of it, regardless of how careful the application firmware is.

<b>1. Post: Security wake-up — the bootloader bug that undermines trust before it's checked</b>
Six new bugs. Zero CVEs assigned. And they all fire before the signature check that's supposed to stop them.

Binarly's research team found six vulnerabilities in U-Boot's FIT image parser — the code that reads a firmware image before verifying its signature. Two allow arbitrary code execution at boot; the rest crash or hang the device. The vulnerable code traces back to 2013, meaning it's shipped in over 50 stable U-Boot releases and every vendor fork built on top.

Here's the part that should reframe how you think about secure boot: the whole point of a signature check is that you don't trust the image until it's verified. These bugs get triggered by the parser doing exactly the untrusted parsing it's supposed to do, before that check ever runs. A "secure boot" chain is only as strong as the code that runs before the crypto does.

If your product boots off U-Boot, do you know which of your board's boot stages actually run before signature verification completes?

<b>5. Post: Regulatory shift — the compliance standard everyone's waiting on isn't ready</b>
The EU wants you to prove CRA compliance. The standard that would make that easy won't exist until the standard-writers say it does — and that's not until late 2026.

EN IEC 62443 is meant to be the harmonized standard that gives manufacturers a "if you meet this, you meet the CRA" shortcut. Industry analysis now puts its finalization at the end of 2026 — which, once it lands, leaves manufacturers under 12 months to prove compliance before the CRA's main December 2027 obligations bite.

For engineers, this matters beyond the compliance team: it means the "CRA-ready" process your company (or a job posting) claims to have today is very likely still improvising against a moving target, because the reference standard itself isn't finished. Understanding that gap — and what CRA-adjacent skills genuinely exist right now versus what's still vaporware — is a real edge when EU embedded hiring starts asking about it.
