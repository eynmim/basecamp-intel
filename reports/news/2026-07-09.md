<b>1. Post: Security wake-up — the login function with a second, undocumented password</b>
A router's login function has a hidden fallback path — and if you fail the real check, that fallback always wins.

CERT/CC's VU#213560 (CVE-2026-11405) describes an undocumented admin bypass in Tenda's httpd: when standard MD5 authentication fails, the firmware quietly compares your submitted password in plaintext against an internal sys.rzadmin.password value, and grants full admin access if it matches — no matter what username you sent. It hits several current firmware builds, and Tenda hasn't responded to disclosure.

The part worth internalizing: no feature test would ever catch this, because the fallback path isn't documented behavior — it only surfaces under source review or fuzzing of the failure branch, not the success branch.

When's the last time you audited what your auth code does after the "correct" check fails, not just what it does when it passes?

https://kb.cert.org/vuls/id/213560

<b>4. Post: Regulatory shift — the FCC wants your BOM to prove its own provenance</b>
The FCC is about to make "we didn't know that chip was on the list" a much harder excuse.

A July 1 fact sheet confirms a draft Third Report and Order — vote expected July 22 — that would bar equipment authorization for any device containing logic-bearing hardware (modules, chips, transceivers) sourced from Covered List entities, not just finished end products.

This pushes supply-chain compliance down to the component level. Specifying a Wi-Fi module or MCU for a US-market product now means Covered-List provenance is a design constraint you check during part selection, not a legal-team afterthought at the end.

If you're speccing components for US-market IoT hardware, is Covered-List provenance already part of your BOM review?

<b>5. Post: Price-floor shift — one chip just deleted nine components from a brake board</b>
One chip just deleted nine external components from a brake-by-wire board.

Allegro's A81415 is the first ASIL-D safety PMIC with an integrated wheel-speed sensor interface, decoding 2-level, PWM, AK, and HD protocols on-chip. Dual watchdogs and AEC-Q100/ASIL-D qualification remove the need for a separate decoder IC and external protection circuitry — roughly $4 BOM cost and over 50% PCB area saved per corner module.

The pattern worth noticing: safety certification used to force related functions onto separate, independently-verifiable ICs. As certification methodology matures, those functions get folded back onto one die — and the savings compound across every corner of the vehicle, four wheels at a time.

Which of your own designs still has a function split across two ICs purely because that's how the safety case used to require it?
