<b>═ 🔥 TOP STORY ═</b>
Security researcher Tod Beardsley at runZero disclosed seven CVEs in FatFs, the FAT/exFAT filesystem library vendored into nearly every embedded stack in daily use: Espressif's ESP-IDF, STMicroelectronics' STM32Cube middleware, Zephyr RTOS, MicroPython, ArduPilot, RT-Thread, Mbed, TizenRT, and SWUpdate. The worst, CVE-2026-6682 (CVSS 7.6), is an integer overflow in FatFs's mount_volume() routine during FAT32 mounting that lets a booby-trapped USB drive, SD card, or firmware-update image hand back attacker-controlled file-size metadata, opening the door to heap or stack overflow and code execution. Six of the seven disclosed CVEs remain unpatched upstream, meaning every vendor who bundles FatFs — including Espressif and ST — has to independently track down and backport a fix rather than pull one from a single upstream release. Why it matters: this isn't a bug in any one RTOS or vendor's own code, it's a bug in the shared, largely invisible filesystem layer sitting underneath ESP-IDF, STM32Cube, and Zephyr alike — exactly the kind of dependency an embedded engineer inherits without ever choosing it, and exactly the kind that doesn't surface until someone plugs in the wrong SD card.

<b>1. Post: Security wake-up — the filesystem code you never chose is now the attack surface</b>
One vulnerable library just became a shared attack surface across ESP-IDF, STM32Cube, Zephyr, and MicroPython at the same time.

runZero disclosed seven CVEs in FatFs, the FAT/exFAT filesystem code vendored into nearly every embedded stack in common use. The worst, CVE-2026-6682 (CVSS 7.6), is an integer overflow in the FAT32 mount routine that a booby-trapped SD card, USB drive, or OTA image can trigger into a heap or stack overflow.

The part worth sitting with: six of the seven CVEs are still unpatched upstream. FatFs isn't something most of us chose — it's a dependency inherited by picking ESP-IDF or STM32Cube, so the fix timeline depends on Espressif and ST backporting independently rather than one upstream release fixing everyone at once.

If your firmware mounts untrusted removable media, when did you last check which FatFs version it's vendoring?

<b>2. Post: Regulatory shift — the EU just put a 24-hour clock on disclosing your own vulnerabilities</b>
Starting September 11, 2026, if you ship a connected product into the EU and it turns out to be under active exploitation, you have 24 hours to tell ENISA.

The European Commission's refreshed Cyber Resilience Act implementation guidance confirms this as the Act's first binding deadline — well ahead of the full product-requirements deadline in December 2027. It applies to manufacturers of "products with digital elements," a category broad enough to cover most connected embedded hardware sold into the EU.

The real implication isn't the reporting itself, it's what the reporting forces upstream: you can't hit a 24-hour disclosure window without exploit-monitoring and an incident pipeline already in place, which makes September's deadline a "build the monitoring now" deadline in disguise.

If you're shipping into the EU, does your team already have a path from "vulnerability discovered" to "ENISA notified" that fits inside a day?

<b>4. Post: Tooling unlock — RISC-V debugging just caught up to Arm's instruction trace</b>
RISC-V targets have long lacked something Arm Cortex-M developers take for granted: vendor-agnostic real-time instruction trace. SEGGER just closed that gap.

J-Link debug probes and J-Trace streaming trace probes now support Andes Technologies' Andes Trace protocol, extending real-time instruction-level trace capture to RISC-V-based embedded targets through the same tooling many of us already use for Arm's ETM.

What changes isn't debugging in the abstract — you could always single-step a RISC-V target — it's post-mortem and timing-sensitive debugging: instruction trace lets you reconstruct exactly what executed leading up to a crash or a missed real-time deadline, instead of reasoning backward from a breakpoint.

As RISC-V shows up in more embedded designs, is instruction-trace parity with Arm the tooling gap that's actually held it back for you?
