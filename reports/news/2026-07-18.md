<b>═ 🔥 TOP STORY ═</b>
Security researcher tokay0 published a working technique showing that a certificate extracted from a single Shark robot vacuum he owns (an RV2320EDUS) can be presented to SharkNinja's AWS IoT broker addressed to any other Shark vacuum's serial number — and the broker accepts it, no scoping check performed. The result is root-level command execution on someone else's device: live camera feed, drive control, the stored home map, and the Wi-Fi password in plaintext. Scanning a single AWS region for 24 hours, tokay0 counted 1,517,605 unique Shark serial numbers, of which 673,816 (44%) answered back confirming they run the vulnerable command handler. SharkNinja has held the report since March 1, acknowledged it March 12, called it "under review" in late April, and promised a fix-completion date for July 10 — then missed it; the flaw was still unpatched when this went public. Why it matters: nothing here is a broken cryptographic primitive — the certificate and signature are both perfectly valid — the failure is architectural, a cloud broker that never checks whether a valid cert actually belongs to the device presenting it, which is exactly the kind of per-device policy scoping mistake that's easy to skip and catastrophic once a fleet ships at scale.

<b>5. Post: Physics-meets-architecture — one harvesting chip, indoor light to full sun</b>
Energy-harvesting PMICs usually get tuned for one light regime. e-peas built one that switches its maximum-power-point strategy based on how bright it actually is.

The AEM15820 pairs low- and high-power boost converters with an MPPT block that runs in open-circuit-ratio mode or constant-voltage mode depending on the photovoltaic cell's operating conditions — the difference between chasing a moving power-point estimate indoors under weak, noisy light and holding a stable voltage target outdoors under strong sun.

The "so what" is fewer BOM variants. A sensor node that ships both indoors and outdoors has historically needed different harvesting front-ends tuned to each light regime; folding both MPPT strategies into one PMIC removes that fork before it starts.

For anyone building solar or indoor-photovoltaic harvesting nodes: would one adaptive PMIC replace two tuned designs in your BOM?
