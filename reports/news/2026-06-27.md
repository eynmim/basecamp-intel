<b>═ 🔥 TOP STORY ═</b>
OpenC6 BIOS (MIT-licensed, GitHub: Rompass/openc6-bios) introduces a PC-style BIOS/payload firmware architecture to the ESP32-C6 RISC-V MCU — a two-layer firmware separation pattern that has no mainstream precedent on MCU-class hardware. The BIOS layer handles hardware initialisation, LP-Core health monitoring, Wi-Fi provisioning, and OTA orchestration, and is never touched during application updates; "payload" programs are small independently-compiled and independently-signed binaries loaded at runtime via a PXE-like Wi-Fi network boot or through the built-in A/B OTA engine with automatic rollback. The engineering consequence is significant: a factory-burned, security-audited BIOS with a stable system-call ABI, and application code that can be updated in the field without rewriting the base system — eliminating the class of OTA failures where a partial write leaves a device permanently unrecoverable. The project currently targets 8 MB Flash ESP32-C6 boards, but the architecture — separate compilation, separate signing, stable ABI, independent rollback partitions — is directly applicable to ESP32-S3 and STM32 multi-stage bootloader designs. As a firmware architectural pattern, it closes the gap between the standard "bootloader + monolithic app" model and a proper two-layer system.

<b>1. Post: [Physics-meets-architecture] — Someone built a BIOS for a $2 RISC-V chip. The architecture is the point.</b>
A developer called Rompass built something I hadn't seen before on an MCU: a BIOS.

OpenC6 BIOS (MIT-licensed, GitHub: Rompass/openc6-bios) separates the ESP32-C6 firmware into a permanent BIOS layer and independently-loadable payload programs. The BIOS handles hardware init, LP-Core health monitoring, Wi-Fi provisioning, and OTA orchestration — and never changes after factory programming. Payloads are small binaries loaded at runtime via a PXE-like Wi-Fi boot or through an A/B OTA engine with automatic rollback, independently compiled and signed without touching the base system.

PC BIOS + OS separation is decades old. It has essentially no precedent in MCU firmware design, where bootloader + monolithic app is the universal pattern.

The consequence: a bricked payload rolls back without recovering the BIOS. A stable, auditable ABI means application updates can be signed independently. The class of OTA failures that leave a device permanently offline shrinks dramatically.

The project targets 8 MB Flash ESP32-C6 boards today. The architecture pattern applies to any MCU with enough flash for two partitions — including ESP32-S3 and STM32.

Is anyone else building firmware with this kind of system/application layer separation?

<b>2. Post: [Security wake-up] — A CVSS 9.2 embedded exploit has been public for 3 days. No patch release yet.</b>
CVE-2026-55200 is a pre-authentication heap overflow in libssh2. A working PoC has been public for three days. There is still no official libssh2 1.11.2 release.

The bug: ssh2_transport_read() lets an attacker supply a packet_length value that wraps a 32-bit integer, underallocating the heap buffer, then writes out-of-bounds using the original large value. No credentials needed. The exploit fires during initial SSH transport negotiation.

The embedded problem: libssh2 is statically linked into routers, NAS firmware, industrial controllers, edge gateways, and OTA update daemons. Every one of those products requires an independent vendor firmware rebuild, re-signing, and OTA deployment. Upstream commit 97acf3d contains the fix. No tagged release.

The attack window is open indefinitely for devices on quarterly or annual firmware cycles — which is most of the embedded fleet.

If your OTA pipeline, remote management daemon, or curl integration links libssh2, cherry-pick 97acf3d today. Waiting for an official release is waiting indefinitely.

<b>5. Post: [Regulatory shift] — The EU CRA gives you 75 days. Your SBOM is the compliance mechanism.</b>
On September 11, 2026, the EU Cyber Resilience Act's reporting obligations go live. Every manufacturer of a connected product sold in the EU must notify ENISA of actively exploited vulnerabilities within 24 hours of becoming aware.

That phrase — "becoming aware" — is where the engineering obligation sits.

The CRA defines your Software Bill of Materials as the mechanism that establishes when you became aware of a vulnerability's impact. No live SBOM means you cannot determine the moment a published CVE first applied to your product. You cannot comply on day one without it.

The CRA requires SPDX or CycloneDX format, covering at minimum top-level dependencies. ESP-IDF generates this with idf.py sbom. Zephyr generates it with west sbom. If neither is wired into your CI pipeline today, you are building a compliance gap that grows with every new CVE.

There is also a direct firmware engineering implication: the CRA explicitly flags unencrypted firmware stored at rest in flash as non-compliant with its data-protection requirement. Flash encryption is now a regulatory checkbox for EU-market products, not an optional hardening step.

75 days to get the monitoring pipeline live.
