<b>═ 🔥 TOP STORY ═</b>

A critical certificate-validation bypass (CVE-2026-5194, CVSS 9.3 NVD / 10.0 Red Hat) was disclosed in the wolfSSL TLS library — the C-native SSL/TLS implementation used across an estimated 5 billion devices including consumer routers, IoT nodes, industrial gateways, and military communications systems. The flaw allows a malicious peer to present a forged digital identity — server certificate, TLS connection, or signed file — that wolfSSL accepts as genuine without triggering any error; authentication silently succeeds against an attacker-controlled endpoint. The patch is available in wolfSSL v5.9.1, but the embedded-specific problem is stark: wolfSSL is almost always statically compiled into firmware images, meaning OS-level security updates do nothing. Every affected firmware binary must be rebuilt against v5.9.1 and pushed through a full OTA update cycle. For devices lacking reliable OTA — the majority of deployed embedded hardware — this vulnerability will remain unpatched in the field for months or years. Any ESP-IDF or STM32-based project that calls wolfSSL for TLS, certificate parsing, or OTA verification should be treated as exposed until confirmed otherwise.

<b>1. Post: [Security wake-up] — wolfSSL CVSS 10: a certificate spoofing bypass in the TLS library of 5 billion embedded devices</b>

The only fix is a rebuild against wolfSSL v5.9.1 and a full OTA push to every affected device in the field. For devices without reliable OTA — most deployed embedded hardware — this remains live exposure indefinitely.

Check your firmware trees for wolfSSL before anything else today. The grep is two seconds; the exposure window if you don't isn't.

<b>3. Post: [Physics-meets-architecture] — Renesas put a 1 GHz Cortex-M85 and a 256 GOPS NPU on the same MCU die. Here is why that combination makes sense.</b>
Running a neural network in under 10 ms on a microcontroller is a constraint problem, not a compute problem.

The Renesas RA8P1 integrates a 1 GHz Arm Cortex-M85 primary core (7,300+ CoreMarks), a 250 MHz Cortex-M33 secondary core, and an Arm Ethos-U55 NPU delivering 256 GOPS at 500 MHz. Benchmarks cover MobileNet, ResNet, DS-CNN, and TinyYOLO.

The architectural insight is the dual-core split: the M33 handles low-power real-time tasks independently — interrupts, sensor polling, BLE event handling — while the M85 stays in a deeper power state between inference bursts. When a burst is needed, M85 wakes, the Ethos-U55 handles the MAC-loop inner loops (its dedicated data paths make data movement 10–100x more efficient than a general-purpose CPU for this pattern), and M85 manages control flow and pre/post-processing. Time-averaged power stays low even though peak compute is high.

High peak compute and low average power are not in conflict on a well-partitioned multi-core design. That is the reason the architecture exists.
