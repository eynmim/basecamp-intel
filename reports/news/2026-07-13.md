<b>═ 🔥 TOP STORY ═</b>
Firmware security firm Binarly disclosed six new vulnerabilities in U-Boot, the bootloader that starts up everything from home routers to smart cameras to the management chips inside data-center servers. Four of the six are crash/DoS bugs, but two — tracked as BRLY-2026-037 and BRLY-2026-038 — can be escalated to arbitrary code execution while U-Boot is still parsing a malicious firmware image, before any signature check runs. Most of the vulnerable code has existed since U-Boot 2013.07, meaning it's potentially present across more than 50 releases and every vendor who forked that code into their own board support package; there is no stable release with the fix yet, so Binarly is telling maintainers to cherry-pick the upstream commits directly instead of waiting. No CVEs have been assigned and there's no known active exploitation, but the timing lands right next to two other boot-chain stories this week — a vendor-signed UEFI Secure Boot bypass (VU#457458) and the FatFs filesystem CVEs from last week — reinforcing that the pre-signature-check parsing code in bootloaders and filesystem libraries is this year's soft underbelly. Why it matters: if you've ever built or flashed anything on an embedded-Linux board (Raspberry Pi, BeagleBone, most SoM/SBC designs), your boot chain's trust model may currently rest on unpatched code with no fix date.

<b>1. Post: Security wake-up — a bootloader bug older than most embedded engineers' careers</b>
Six new bugs just surfaced in code that's been sitting in bootloaders since 2013 — and there's still no fixed release.

Binarly disclosed six U-Boot flaws this week. Four crash devices, but two let a malicious firmware image run arbitrary code while U-Boot is still parsing it — before the bootloader has even checked the image's signature. Most of the vulnerable code dates back to U-Boot 2013.07, meaning it's been silently forked into 50+ releases and countless vendor board-support packages since.

The part that should bother you more than the bugs themselves: there's no CVE, no upstream fixed release, and Binarly's advice to maintainers is "don't wait, cherry-pick our commits directly." That's not a patch pipeline, that's a workaround for the absence of one — in code that decides whether your board boots something you trust.

If your product ships with U-Boot, do you actually know which fork and which year's code you're running?

<b>2. Post: Physics-meets-architecture — a sensor chip with no wires to a power supply</b>
Three layers, 50 nanometers apart, and not one of them needs a battery.

Researchers at Penn State built a chip that stacks a silicon photovoltaic layer, 2D-semiconductor logic (MoS₂/WSe₂ transistors), and graphene chemical sensors directly on top of each other. Ambient light powers the whole stack — the same photons that hit the bottom layer end up powering the sensing and the on-chip digital classification of what was sensed.

What's architecturally interesting isn't just "it's self-powered" — it's that there's no discrete PMIC stage between harvesting and compute. The energy harvesting layer feeds the logic layer directly through vertical interconnects, collapsing a chain that normally spans three separate ICs (harvester, PMIC, MCU) into one 3D monolithic stack.

Curious how far "harvest, compute, sense, no PMIC" scales before you're back to needing power conditioning for reliability, not just efficiency.

<b>3. Post: Regulatory shift — the FCC just moved the security perimeter down to the chip level</b>
Until now, a banned vendor's name on the outer casing was the thing regulators checked. That's about to stop being sufficient.

The FCC's July 1 proposal would close what it calls the "component part loophole": any device containing a logic-bearing hardware component from a Covered List entity — not just complete equipment badged by one — would become ineligible for US equipment authorization. Online marketplaces would also have to display the FCC ID at the point of sale.

The engineering consequence is real: BOM-level supply chain provenance stops being a paperwork exercise for compliance teams and starts being something you need to track per-component, because a single sourced chip inside an otherwise "clean" design could now sink the whole product's certification.

If you had to prove the provenance of every IC on your last board's BOM today, could you?

A non-cellular 5G mesh protocol that can scale to a million nodes, and Norik just made trying it as easy as plugging in a USB stick.

Their new dongle wraps Nordic's nRF9151 SiP — Cortex-M33, DECT NR+, LTE-M/NB-IoT, GNSS — in a USB-C form factor with a built-in CMSIS-DAP debugger and a pre-flashed console shell.
