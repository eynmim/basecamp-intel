<b>1. Post: [Security wake-up] — OpenSSL CVSS 9.8 lives inside your firmware binary and apt won't fix it</b>
Your package manager cannot patch this one.

CVE-2026-45447, disclosed June 9, is a heap use-after-free in OpenSSL's PKCS7_verify(). A crafted PKCS#7 or S/MIME message with an empty ASN.1 SET in the digestAlgorithms field causes the function to free a BIO object it doesn't own. The calling application still holds a live reference. What follows is heap corruption — potential RCE, CVSS 9.8.

The embedded-specific problem: most TLS-capable firmware statically links OpenSSL. Industrial gateways, BLE-to-cloud bridges, MQTT-over-TLS nodes — they typically bundle a compiled-in libssl. When the OS gets the OpenSSL patch, the firmware binary does not. The only fix is a rebuild-and-OTA-update cycle for every affected device in the field.

Across seven affected release branches (1.0.2 through 3.6), the majority of shipped IoT firmware is currently exposed.

How many OpenSSL versions are running across your deployed fleet right now?

<b>2. Post: [Capability unlock] — A $1 MCU just ran neural inference 90x faster than the chip it replaces</b>

The MSPM0G5187 is a Cortex-M0+ at 80 MHz with 128 KB flash and 32 KB SRAM. Not fast silicon. The reason the numbers are so dramatic isn't magic — it's data movement. A general-purpose CPU running an inference loop spends most of its energy and cycles loading weight tensors through a pipeline optimised for random-access computation, not the tight MAC-loop pattern of neural networks. The TinyEngine NPU has dedicated data paths for exactly that pattern. The CPU doesn't slow down; it stays idle while the NPU handles the inner loop.

Until this month, adding ML to a $0.80 sensor node meant a separate AI co-processor and a second set of firmware complexity. That constraint is gone.

<b>5. Post: [Physics-meets-architecture] — STM32 runs neural inference on indoor light. No battery. Here's why it works.</b>
ST demonstrated this at Embedded World: a Nucleo board running a person-detection model continuously, powered by a small solar panel. No battery in the circuit.

The chip is the STM32U3C5, a Cortex-M33 at 96 MHz with 2 MB flash and 640 KB SRAM. The headline number: 117 CoreMark/mW in active mode, operating down to 0.65 V at near-threshold voltage. A Hardware Signal Processor (HSP) accelerator delivers 13x the AI inference performance of a bare Cortex-M33 and 9x better efficiency than STM32U5.

The physics: near-threshold design shrinks the gap between supply voltage and transistor threshold, reducing dynamic power quadratically. The tradeoff is process variation sensitivity — tolerances that don't matter at 1.8 V become critical at 0.65 V, which is why you don't see every MCU at this voltage. The HSP then concentrates the remaining energy budget on the specific computation pattern that matters: matrix multiply-accumulate for neural inference.

The result is that the energy cost per inference drops below what a typical indoor PV cell delivers on an overcast day.

The bottleneck for TinyML was never FLOPS. It was always energy per useful operation.
