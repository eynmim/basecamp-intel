CVE-2025-68473 discloses an out-of-bounds write in the BlueDroid Bluetooth SDP implementation shipped with ESP-IDF. <b>1. Post: Security wake-up — The 32-service assumption that breaks ESP32 Bluetooth memory</b>
There is a hardcoded 32-service limit in the ESP32 Bluetooth stack. Send a device with 33 services and you get a write past the end of the buffer.

CVE-2025-68473 (GHSA-hmjj-rjvv-w8pq, CVSS 6.1) describes an out-of-bounds write in BlueDroid's bta_dm_sdp_result(): when a peer BLE device advertises more than 32 services, the ESP32 Bluetooth host writes beyond a fixed-size uuid_list array. On bare-metal FreeRTOS with no MMU, heap corruption from a malformed SDP response can crash the device — or, depending on heap layout, corrupt control data.

The engineering detail: this is not an exotic vulnerability class. It is a fixed buffer with no boundary check — the same pattern that has recurred in every generation of embedded Bluetooth stacks. The assumption "we only encounter devices with fewer than 32 services" was never documented, never tested, and never enforced. Espressif's fix replaces the hardcoded limit with a configurable constant and adds a bounds check. Patched in ESP-IDF v5.5.1, v5.4.3, v5.3.4, v5.2.6, and v5.1.6.

If your project uses ESP-IDF and initiates BLE SDP discovery: check your version today.

<b>3. Post: Capability unlock — INT4 quantization is now in TFLite Micro. What that actually changes.</b>
INT8 quantization halved model size versus float32. INT4 halves it again. In 2026 it is shipping in TFLite Micro — with one important caveat.

INT4 weight quantization is production-available in TFLite Micro for the most common operators: Conv2D, depthwise convolutions, fully connected layers. LSTM and RNN ops lag behind — operator coverage is partial. But those three operator types cover the majority of production TinyML workloads: keyword spotting, image classification, anomaly detection.

The concrete impact on ESP32-S3: a 200 KB INT8 keyword-spotter becomes roughly 100 KB in INT4, fitting in half the flash and cutting PSRAM bandwidth during inference. The S3's 128-bit vector extension processes 4-bit weights with no hardware changes — the improvement is real and measurable.

The tradeoff is accuracy. INT4 introduces quantization error. On well-trained models with sufficient calibration data, accuracy drops are typically 1–3% on classification tasks. On small models or noisy training sets, the gap can be larger. Validate on your data, not just on latency benchmarks.

<b>5. Post: Physics-meets-architecture — 5 µW at 275 mV. What that cold-start number means in practice.</b>
Most energy-harvesting datasheets bury the cold-start spec. The e-peas AEM15820 leads with it: 5 µW at 275 mV open-circuit voltage.

Here is why that number matters. Cold start is the state where all storage is depleted and the PMIC must boot from harvested power alone. At 275 mV from an indoor photovoltaic cell, a standard PMIC would not start — the threshold is too low. The AEM15820 starts here. That means the system can recover from a fully discharged state under typical office ambient lighting, with no pre-charged supercapacitor and no primary battery as a backup reservoir.

The AEM15820 is also the first PMIC designed for hybrid PV cells that span both indoor and outdoor light levels. A single IC manages PV input from microwatts under dim indoor light to several watts in direct sunlight, without the designer specifying a separate management path for each condition.

The architecture this enables: a maintenance-free sensor node that wakes from dead-empty in an office, completes one measurement and transmission, and sleeps again — indefinitely, with no physical intervention.

For building automation, asset tracking, and industrial sensing where battery replacement is the operational bottleneck, this closes a real gap.
