<b>═ 🔥 TOP STORY ═</b>
Security firm runZero disclosed on July 1 that seven bugs in FatFs — the tiny, near-universal FAT/exFAT filesystem library — have no upstream fix, because its sole maintainer and Japan's JPCERT/CC coordination center could not get a response after repeated outreach. The headline bugs are CVE-2026-6682 (CVSS 7.6, an integer overflow while mounting a FAT32 volume that turns a bad file-size computation into a real read length) and CVE-2026-6687 (CVSS 7.6, an exFAT volume-label field overflow), both giving an attacker who plants a booby-trapped USB drive, SD card, or update file a memory-corruption foothold. runZero explicitly names Espressif ESP-IDF and STMicroelectronics STM32Cube as affected middleware, alongside Zephyr, MicroPython, ArduPilot, RT-Thread, Mbed, Samsung TizenRT, and the SWUpdate updater — and has already published PoC disk images and a working QEMU exploit harness. There is no coordinated patch process and no security mailing list for the library, so anyone shipping FatFs (directly or bundled inside a vendor SDK) needs to audit and harden their own mount/read paths rather than wait for an update.

<b>1. Post: Security wake-up — the filesystem library nobody's maintaining</b>
One unmaintained filesystem library is quietly running inside ESP-IDF, STM32Cube, Zephyr, MicroPython, ArduPilot, and half a dozen other embedded stacks — and its maintainer hasn't answered anyone in months.

That's the situation runZero disclosed on July 1: seven bugs in FatFs, the tiny FAT/exFAT library nearly every RTOS bundles for SD-card and USB support. The headline bug is an integer overflow while mounting a FAT32 volume — bad math produces a false file size, and later code treats that false size as a real read length, which on real hardware becomes memory corruption.

Six of the seven bugs have no upstream fix. Not "a slow fix" — no fix, no security mailing list, and a single maintainer that even Japan's JPCERT/CC couldn't reach.

If your firmware touches an SD card, USB drive, or update image through FatFs, have you actually audited what happens on a malformed volume — or just trusted it because it's always been there?

https://www.runzero.com/blog/fatfs-bugs/

<b>2. Post: Integration leap — post-quantum crypto lands in a secure element</b>
STMicroelectronics just put a post-quantum cryptography accelerator inside the same chip that already handles NFC, eSIM, and secure-element duties for phones.

The ST54M combines all of that with certified software libraries (NesLib-PQML, X-CUBE-PQC) that are also usable on STM32 microcontrollers, and it's hardened against side-channel and fault-injection attacks — not just "PQC math added," but PQC math protected the way ECC/RSA already were.

That's the part worth sitting with: post-quantum algorithms have very different power and timing signatures than the crypto they're replacing, so bolting them onto a secure element isn't a firmware-only migration — it needs fresh countermeasure design at the silicon level.

How many embedded teams budgeting for the PQC transition are actually planning for that, versus assuming it's a drop-in library swap?

<b>3. Post: Capability unlock — RISC-V standardizes matrix math</b>
RISC-V just moved toward a standardized way to do matrix math in hardware — and that quietly ends a fragmentation problem most embedded engineers haven't noticed yet.

At RISC-V Summit Europe 2026 in Bologna, RISC-V International discussed new Matrix extensions for ML acceleration alongside hardware security extensions, part of the broader RVA23 profile push tying the ecosystem together.

Until now, every RISC-V AI accelerator vendor rolled its own custom matrix instructions, meaning compilers and inference runtimes had to be hand-tuned per vendor. A standardized extension means frameworks can finally target one instruction set instead of a dozen forks — the same trade-off Arm made with Helium/MVE years ago.

Worth learning RISC-V vector/matrix intrinsics now, or better to wait for the tooling to catch up?

<b>4. Post: Tooling unlock — inference without an accelerator</b>
ONNX Runtime can now use RISC-V's vector extension on the CPU execution provider — no NPU, no accelerator SDK required.

The RVV support landed in v1.26, with v1.27 continuing quantization work like 2-bit QMoE and SIMD-optimized quantized KV-cache.

The real unlock here: RISC-V MCUs and application processors without a dedicated NPU can now get meaningful inference speedups from vectorized software ops instead of waiting on a vendor-specific accelerator driver. That drops the bar for RISC-V edge-AI experimentation from "did the vendor ship an SDK" to "does your core implement RVV."

<b>5. Post: Regulatory shift — the 24-hour clock starts in September</b>
September 11, 2026 is the date EU cyber-incident reporting stops being "someday" and becomes a 24-hour clock.

Under the EU Cyber Resilience Act's Article 14, manufacturers of connected digital products will have to report actively-exploited vulnerabilities to ENISA within 24 hours of becoming aware of them, followed by a 72-hour update and a final report at 14 days to one month — well ahead of full CRA conformity requirements in December 2027.

The practical engineering consequence: you need a triage-and-reporting pipeline built and rehearsed months in advance. Twenty-four hours is not enough time to invent your incident-response process for the first time under pressure.

If you ship connected hardware into the EU, does your team already have a drill for this, or is September going to be the first real test?
