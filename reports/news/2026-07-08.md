<b>═ 🔥 TOP STORY ═</b>
Security firm runZero disclosed seven CVEs (CVE-2026-6682 through CVE-2026-6688) in FatFs, the tiny FAT/exFAT filesystem driver vendored into nearly every embedded stack: Espressif ESP-IDF, STMicroelectronics STM32Cube, Zephyr, MicroPython, ArduPilot, RT-Thread, Mbed, and Samsung TizenRT. The headline bug, CVE-2026-6682 (CVSS 7.6), is an integer overflow in mount_volume() that lets a crafted FAT32 volume forge file-size metadata trusted by downstream code, opening the door to heap or stack corruption; a second high-severity bug abuses oversized exFAT/long-filename fields to overflow fixed-size caller buffers. Six of the seven have no upstream fix at all — FatFs has no security mailing list and no mechanism for the hundreds of products bundling it to even learn they're affected — and two of the bugs are reachable through OTA firmware-update parsing, not just inserted SD cards or USB sticks. This is squarely in Ali's stack: any ESP32-S3 or STM32 project that reads a FAT-formatted SD card, USB drive, or firmware image inherits this until Espressif or ST ship a patched vendored copy.

<b>1. Post: Security wake-up — seven bugs in the filesystem every MCU trusts</b>
Six of the seven new FatFs CVEs have no fix. Not "patch pending" — no fix, no security mailing list, no way for most vendors to even know they're affected.

FatFs is the tiny FAT/exFAT driver vendored into ESP-IDF, STM32Cube, Zephyr, MicroPython, ArduPilot, and half a dozen other embedded stacks. runZero's headline bug, CVE-2026-6682, is an integer overflow in mount_volume() — a crafted FAT32 volume forges file-size metadata that downstream code trusts as a read length, and you get heap or stack corruption.

What actually worries me: two of the seven are reachable through OTA firmware-update parsing, not just a malicious SD card. That turns "don't plug in untrusted USB sticks" into a much smaller mitigation than it sounds.

If your ESP32 or STM32 project mounts anything FAT-formatted, this is worth checking today.

<b>2. Post: Capability unlock — one chip, two AI domains that never talk over a shared bus</b>
Infineon's new PSOC Edge E84 kit ships with two separate compute domains on one die, and that split is the actual feature.

A 400MHz Cortex-M55 with Helium and an Ethos-U55 NPU handles the heavy inference — vision, audio classification, whatever needs real throughput. Sitting alongside it, a low-power Cortex-M33 paired with Infineon's NNLite accelerator stays always-on, watching for a wake event at near-zero power.

The insight: this isn't one MCU doing double duty on a power budget. It's an architectural bet that "always listening" and "actually understanding" are different enough workloads to deserve physically separate cores, so the expensive silicon only wakes up when the cheap silicon says something's there.

Where else could this always-on/high-performance split replace a single do-everything core in your own designs?

<b>3. Post: Physics-meets-architecture — running AI on a chip that operates below 1V</b>
ST's STM32U3B5/C5 runs its core logic down to 0.65V. For reference, most MCU logic is designed to switch cleanly well above 1V — this is deliberately near the threshold voltage where transistors barely turn on at all.

The payoff for operating that close to the edge: 117 CoreMark/mW, plus an onboard HSP accelerator that lets the chip run actual ML inference — not just sleep and wait — while living on nothing but harvested energy.

The engineering trade nobody talks about enough: near-threshold design buys you huge efficiency, but leakage current and process variation get proportionally nastier the closer you push to the edge. ST shipping this in a production part, not a research paper, says that trade-off is now manageable at volume.

Would you design a harvested-power sensor node around inference-capable silicon, or keep the AI off-node and just report raw data?

<b>5. Post: Tooling unlock — a decade-old RTOS just went read-only, on schedule</b>
Arm announced Mbed OS's end-of-life back in 2024. This month, it actually happens: the Mbed website archives, the GitHub repo freezes, and no more fixes come from Arm — ever.

Mbed OS was, for a long stretch, the default on-ramp for STM32 development: browser-based IDE, huge board library, low barrier to entry. That's genuinely over now, not "deprecated but still limping along" — it's archived.

The interesting part isn't the sunset itself, it's the fork: Mbed CE, a community-maintained continuation, is where the actual maintenance now happens. It's a live example of what a widely-used embedded platform does when its corporate steward walks away — the community either forks or the ecosystem just stops.

If you've got old Mbed OS projects sitting around, this is the actual deadline to move them.
