<b>═ 🔥 TOP STORY ═</b>
Security researchers at runZero disclosed seven new CVEs (CVE-2026-6682 through CVE-2026-6688) in FatFs, the tiny FAT/exFAT filesystem library vendored into nearly every embedded SDK and RTOS in use today — Espressif's ESP-IDF, STMicro's STM32Cube, Zephyr, MicroPython, ArduPilot, RT-Thread, Samsung's TizenRT, and the SWUpdate OTA tool all bundle it. The worst bug, CVE-2026-6682, is an integer overflow in the FAT32 mount routine that lets a crafted disk image or malicious OTA payload forge a file size and turn it into a memory-corruption primitive; CVE-2026-6687 is a stack overflow in f_getlabel(). Only one of the seven — CVE-2026-6684 — has an upstream fix, shipped in FatFs R0.16; the other six remain unpatched because FatFs has a single maintainer, no security mailing list, and no coordinated way to notify the many downstream projects that vendor its source directly instead of tracking it as a dependency. Public proof-of-concept disk images already exist. Why it matters: if you've ever pointed f_mount() at a USB stick, SD card, or OTA image you didn't fully control — on an ESP32, STM32, or Zephyr board — that's now part of your threat model, whether your SDK vendor has said anything about it yet or not.

<b>1. Post: Security wake-up — one maintainer, six unpatched bugs, everyone's filesystem</b>
One person maintains the filesystem library sitting inside your ESP32, your STM32, and probably your drone's autopilot too.

runZero just disclosed seven CVEs in FatFs, the FAT/exFAT library vendored into ESP-IDF, STM32Cube, Zephyr, MicroPython, ArduPilot, RT-Thread, and TizenRT. The worst one is an integer overflow in the FAT32 mount routine — a crafted SD card or OTA image can forge a file size and turn it into memory corruption.

Only one of the seven bugs has an upstream fix. The other six are stuck because FatFs has no security mailing list and no coordinated way to reach the dozens of projects that copy its source directly instead of tracking it as a dependency — this is what "vendored, not pinned" risk actually looks like at scale.

If your firmware ever mounts a filesystem from media you don't fully control, do you actually know which FatFs version is in your build?

<b>2. Post: Regulatory shift — secure boot just stopped being optional</b>
Firmware signature verification and secure boot are no longer best practice for industrial control certification. They're now the certification requirement.

IEC 62443-4-2:2026, the revised standard governing IACS component security, makes signed firmware, secure boot, and OTA rollback capability mandatory for PLCs, edge controllers, and gateways seeking certification — enforcement starts on new submissions in Q3 2026.

The engineering angle: certification bodies are reporting certification timelines stretching from about five weeks to eight-to-twelve. That's not paperwork friction — it's a sign that a lot of existing product boot chains aren't actually built to prove what the standard now demands, and retrofitting secure boot after the fact is a much harder problem than designing it in from day one.

If you're building anything industrial-adjacent, is your boot chain already provable, or would you be starting from scratch?

<b>3. Post: Price-floor shift — nine components and $4 of BOM, gone into one chip</b>
Nine external components and roughly four dollars of BOM cost per vehicle. That's what Allegro just folded into a single PMIC.

The A81415 is billed as the first ASIL-D safety PMIC with a built-in wheel-speed sensor interface — it decodes 2-level PWM and 3-level AK sensor protocols on-chip, and integrates a buck-boost pre-regulator plus five LDOs in a single-inductor architecture with no external switches or diodes.

The interesting part isn't just the cost cut, it's what gets absorbed into silicon: sensor-protocol decoding used to be a discrete signal-conditioning problem sitting between the sensor and the MCU. When it moves inside the safety PMIC itself, the board layout, the safety case, and the BOM all get simpler at once — that's a genuine architecture shift, not just a cheaper part.

Anyone else seeing sensor-interface logic migrate into power ICs on their own designs?

<b>4. Post: Capability unlock — $39 gets you a RISC-V softcore you program in Lua</b>
A RISC-V core, an FPGA, and a scripting language, on one $39 Feather board.

BrisbaneSilicon's ELM11-Feather runs a PicoRV32 RISC-V softcore at up to 75MHz on a Gowin GW1NR FPGA — but instead of a C toolchain, you program it natively in Lua through EmbluaOS, with spare FPGA fabric left over if you want to drop down to custom RTL.

What makes this worth a second look: it collapses the usual "pick one" tradeoff between fast-iteration scripting and close-to-metal RTL access into a single cheap board. You get a softcore you can actually reconfigure, not just a fixed RISC-V MCU, plus a scripting layer for the parts of a project where C's edit-compile-flash loop is overkill.

Would you reach for something like this over a standard RISC-V MCU for a fast-prototyping project?
