<b>1. Post: Security wake-up — The ECDSA library trusted by 5 billion devices couldn't count bytes</b>
wolfSSL's certificate verification accepted signatures with truncated digests. That sentence is CVSS 10.0.

CVE-2026-5194 in wolfSSL &lt; 5.9.1: the library verified that an ECDSA/DSA/ML-DSA/Ed25519/Ed448 signature was mathematically consistent with the certificate, but never checked that the digest was the correct number of bytes. An attacker could present a forged certificate with a deliberately short hash — and the device would accept it as legitimate.

The engineering consequence: TLS mutual authentication fails silently. A device running unpatched wolfSSL cannot distinguish between a real server and an attacker replaying a crafted short-hash certificate. Every encrypted connection becomes decryptable by the attacker, with no warning, no error, no log entry.

wolfSSL is embedded in ESP-IDF, FreeRTOS+TCP, automotive stacks, SOHO routers, and military hardware. The fix (version 5.9.1, April 8, 2026) is available. Most of those 5 billion devices will never receive it. That's not a wolfSSL problem — it's the firmware update problem at scale.

If your embedded project uses wolfSSL for HTTPS or MQTT-TLS, check your pinned version. The check takes 30 seconds.
