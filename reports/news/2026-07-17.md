<b>═ 🔥 TOP STORY ═</b>
ESET researchers disclosed that 11 legacy UEFI shim bootloaders (versions ≤0.9), all still trusted under the widely deployed "Microsoft Corporation UEFI CA 2011" certificate, can be dropped onto virtually any UEFI system to defeat Secure Boot outright — no zero-day required, just an attacker-supplied copy of an old, already-signed shim chained to a vulnerable second-stage loader such as GRUB2 (CVE-2015-5281) or a newly tracked certificate-length parsing flaw, CVE-2026-10797. The issue is now tracked by CERT/CC as VU#616257 (covering CVE-2026-8863 and CVE-2026-10797), and while Microsoft revoked the specific vulnerable hashes in its June 9 dbx (forbidden-signature database) update, ESET notes nobody has an authoritative count of how many other unrevoked, decade-old but still-signed shims remain in circulation. This lands two weeks after the U-Boot FIT pre-auth bugs covered here on July 16, making it the second major boot-chain trust story in as many weeks. Why it matters: Secure Boot's trust model assumes every signed binary in the chain is safe, but a decade of shims signed under one long-lived CA means "signed" and "safe" have quietly diverged — and the fix (dbx revocation) is reactive, per-hash, and permanently behind whatever old shim an attacker digs up next.

<b>1. Post: Security wake-up — the "signed" boot files nobody revoked</b>
"Signed" and "safe" just came apart for Secure Boot, and the flaw isn't a new exploit — it's arithmetic on old files.

ESET found that 11 old Microsoft-signed UEFI shim versions (≤0.9) are still valid under the decade-old "Microsoft Corporation UEFI CA 2011" certificate. Chain one of those shims to a known-vulnerable second-stage loader — GRUB2's CVE-2015-5281, or a newly tracked certificate-parsing bug, CVE-2026-10797 — and Secure Boot verifies every individually-signed file in the chain while missing that the chain itself was never supposed to still work.

Microsoft revoked the specific bad hashes in June's dbx update, but that's reactive and per-hash. Nobody, including ESET, has an authoritative count of how many other ancient, still-signed shims are sitting in firmware images right now.

If your threat model treats "signature valid" as "component safe," when did you last check how much technical debt is hiding behind the signing cert itself?

<b>2. Post: Physics-meets-architecture — a chip that harvests its own light, computes, and senses chemicals</b>
No battery anywhere in the stack — and that's the whole point of how it's built.

Penn State researchers, publishing in Nature Electronics, built a monolithic 3D stack with three functional layers roughly 50 nanometers apart: a silicon photovoltaic layer for ambient-light harvesting, a middle layer of logic built from 2D semiconductors (n-type MoS2, p-type WSe2), and a top layer of graphene-based chemical sensors.

The interesting part isn't "solar-powered sensor" — that's old news. It's that harvesting, compute, and sensing are fabricated as one vertical stack instead of three dies wired together on a board, which is what actually gets you a device that runs on ambient light with no battery at all. It's a lab proof-of-concept, not a part number — next steps are scaling the 2D CMOS and adding wireless connectivity.

When compute and energy harvesting share the same 50nm of silicon instead of a PCB trace, what's the first battery-free sensor node you'd want to build with it?

<b>5. Post: Regulatory shift — a new baseline for what "secure" means in a PLC</b>
IEC just published the standard that decides which security features your next industrial controller design is required to have.

IEC 62443-4-2:2026 sets the formal component-level security requirements for industrial automation and control systems — authentication, hardening, and secure-communication baselines that PLCs, remote I/O, and embedded controllers increasingly need to meet to win OT/ICS deals in the EU and beyond.

This isn't a paperwork update. Component-level requirements like this are what procurement teams start writing directly into RFPs, and what OT vendors start requiring subcontractors to prove compliance with — which means "we followed 62443-4-2" becomes a real line on an embedded engineer's resume, not just a compliance officer's checkbox.

If you're building anything that could end up behind a PLC or industrial gateway, is 62443-4-2 already on your reading list?
