<b>═ 🔥 TOP STORY ═</b>
CISA and Rockwell Automation disclosed CVE-2026-10577, a maximum-severity flaw (CVSS 10.0 on both the 3.1 and 4.0 scales) in the 1715-AENTR EtherNet/IP Redundant I/O Adapter used across energy, water, and manufacturing plant floors. The adapter exposes a network-reachable debug port with no privilege enforcement, so an unauthenticated attacker on the same network segment can run intrusive CLI commands to read or delete files, halt tasks, modify memory, and directly flip I/O states — meaning the physical signals driving valves, actuators, and drive systems. Firmware 3.003 and earlier are affected; the fix is firmware 3.011. Why it matters: a perfect 10.0 with zero privileges and zero user interaction required is as bad as ICS vulnerabilities get, and it lands the same week as a second Rockwell Logix flaw (CVE-2025-12011, CVSS 9.2) that can crash CompactLogix/ControlLogix controllers outright — a reminder that "debug" and "diagnostic" interfaces left reachable on a production network are still the most common way OT gets popped.

<b>1. Post: Security wake-up — a perfect 10.0 on an I/O adapter you've probably never heard of</b>
CVSS 10.0 is rare. This week Rockwell Automation and CISA disclosed one on a device that directly drives valves and actuators.

CVE-2026-10577 exposes a network-reachable debug port on the 1715-AENTR EtherNet/IP Redundant I/O Adapter with zero privilege enforcement. No authentication, no user interaction, full remote access to an intrusive CLI — read or delete files, kill tasks, rewrite memory, and directly flip I/O states.

The part that should stick: "I/O state" here isn't an abstraction. It's the electrical signal telling a valve to open or an actuator to move on a factory floor or in a water plant. A debug interface nobody remembered to gate became the entire attack surface.

If your design has a debug or diagnostic port, is it actually unreachable from the production network, or just unadvertised?

<b>2. Post: Capability unlock (the uncomfortable kind) — malware that ports itself across 17 architectures</b>
An IoT botnet just shipped with its own reasoning left in the comments. Not obfuscated. Left in, verbatim.

TuxBot v3 Evolution is a modular DDoS framework spanning 17 CPU architectures, carrying 1,496 Telnet credential pairs and exploits for 30+ IoT device families. Researchers found C source files containing raw LLM chain-of-thought — the model's own self-interruptions and decisions as it ported exploit code from one architecture to the next.

The capability that's new isn't the botnet itself, IoT botnets are old news. It's that porting exploit code across a dozen-plus architectures used to be the tedious part that limited how fast these things could spread. An LLM doing that grunt work at scale changes the economics of building one.

Still running default Telnet credentials on anything in your lab network?

<b>3. Post: Integration leap — Matter just made Bluetooth optional for onboarding</b>
Every Matter device so far has needed BLE at some point in setup, even if it runs on Wi-Fi permanently after. That assumption just broke.

Matter 1.4.2 adds Wi-Fi Unsynchronized Service Discovery (USD) as a commissioning path, letting a device join a Matter network using Wi-Fi alone — no BLE handoff step required.

The interesting part for anyone designing radio hardware: BLE-then-Wi-Fi commissioning wasn't just a UX choice, it shaped BOM decisions, since a Wi-Fi-only product still needed a BLE radio just to get through setup. Removing that requirement means a Wi-Fi-only SoC can be a first-class Matter citizen instead of needing to borrow a second radio for ten seconds of pairing.

Would you drop a BLE radio from a Wi-Fi Matter design if commissioning genuinely didn't need it anymore?

<b>4. Post: Regulatory shift — Brussels just gave embedded AI a 12-month exemption, but not the part you'd expect</b>
The EU AI Act just moved a deadline for embedded products by a full year. The catch is it moved the wrong one to relax on.

The Digital Omnibus, signed July 8, pushes the compliance deadline for AI embedded in regulated products (medical devices, radio equipment, toys, and other Annex I categories) from August 2027 to August 2028. That's real breathing room for anyone building inference into a certified product.

What doesn't move: Article 50's transparency obligations, AI-content disclosure and synthetic-content marking, still go live on schedule, August 2, 2026. So the deep compliance work on embedded AI safety cases got a year of slack, while the "tell the user an AI touched this" requirement didn't.

If your project embeds an AI feature, do you know which of these two clocks it's actually running against?

<b>5. Post: Price-floor shift — a PMIC just deleted nine components from a brake-by-wire design</b>
Automotive brake-by-wire has historically meant a wheel-speed decoder chip, a separate PMIC, and the discrete circuitry to tie them together. Allegro just folded all of it into one part.

The A81415 is the industry's first ASIL-D-certified PMIC with an on-chip wheel-speed sensor interface, decoding 2-level, 2-level PWM, and 3-level AK protocols natively alongside a buck-boost pre-regulator and five LDOs. Allegro puts the savings at roughly $4 in BOM cost and nine eliminated external components per vehicle.

The "so what" isn't the dollar figure, it's what integrating the sensor decode into the power chip does to the safety-critical loop: less analog signal chain between sensor and MCU means less latency in a control loop where latency is the entire point.

When does integrating sensing into power management stop being a nice-to-have and start being the obvious default?
